Reading the bands. Above 1.5 is comfortable — shift your effort to health documentation. Between 1.0 and 1.5 is thin and needs liquid assets and a joint sponsor. Below 1.0, treat a refusal as the likely outcome on the current facts and speak to counsel about whether to proceed as things stand.
What the checkpoints do The statutory test is gross household income against 125% of the poverty guideline, and nothing in the checkpoint groups changes it — that comparison stays in the top row. What the checkpoints produce is the second figure the officer builds in their head: whether the household actually survives its first year. Family health coverage is usually the largest single line. If the employer covers only the worker, the cost of adding a spouse and children lands on you and widens the gap; if coverage extends to the family, that line disappears entirely. Employer-provided housing, guaranteed overtime and documented relative support work the same way in the opposite direction. Enter your own figures — the worksheet asserts no default costs, because premiums, rent and childcare vary too much by employer and metro for a generic number to be worth anything.
Weeks worked matters as much as hours. Many Other Worker certifications are seasonal. A 40-hour offer across 40 weeks is not a 40-hour offer, and the officer will read the ETA-9089 and employer letter for exactly that. If the work is year-round, get the employer to say so in writing.
What the worksheet derives for you. Annual wage from your hourly rate, weekly hours and weeks per year. The poverty guideline for your household size, selected automatically from the 2026 HHS tables once you pick a state — the contiguous tier, Alaska, or Hawaii. The shortfall or margin in both annual and monthly terms. And a cash cushion measured against a subsistence baseline of 100% of the poverty guideline divided by twelve, which is a floor for what a household costs to run, not a local cost-of-living estimate. Your actual costs will be higher in a high-rent metro.
Why the assets test sits in its own panel The five-times-the-shortfall rule comes from 8 CFR 213a.2, which governs the Affidavit of Support. A standard EB-3 Other Worker case requires no I-864, so that test does not bind you and there is no assets threshold you must reach. It becomes a real requirement in only one situation: where a relative filed the I-140, or a relative holds 5% or more of the petitioning employer, in which case INA 212(a)(4)(D) mandates an I-864. And it becomes a persuasion benchmark, though not a legal one, if you choose to file a voluntary I-134 or I-864 — because an officer will read that form against the standards it was built around. The scenario selector puts you in the right one of those three worlds. In the default case, savings are evidence of self-sufficiency, not a test to be passed.
How to read the bond estimate There is no published bond schedule. Neither DOS nor USCIS publishes a formula, and consular bonds are vanishingly rare in practice. This estimate deliberately does not borrow the affidavit-of-support assets rule. It carries your annual shortfall across an exposure period you choose, subtracts liquid savings, adjusts for the factors you ticked, then floors at the current USCIS minimum and caps at the figure under discussion. Every element of that is a planning assumption with no legal basis. Treat the result as a number to have accessible, not a prediction of what any officer would demand.
